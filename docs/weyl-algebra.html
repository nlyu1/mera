2 Weyl Algebra

Definition 2.1 (Weyl algebra) The Weyl algebra \(W_p\) and one site is finitely generated by \(U_{(p)}, V_{(p)}\), subject to \[ U^p = V^p = 1, \quad VU = \omega UV \] It generates the Schwinger basis indexed by \(\xi = (q, p) \in \mathbb Z^p\times \mathbb Z^p\): \[ J_\xi = \bar \omega^{\xi_1\xi_2/2} V^{q} U^p \] The \(N\)-site Weyl algebra is defined by \(W_p(N)=W_P^{\otimes n}\).

For example, for \(p=2\) we obtain the Pauli basis \(V=\sigma_x, U=\sigma_z\).

Proposition 2.1 (Weyl Lie relation) Let \(\xi=(q, p)\) equipped with addition modulo \(p\) and \(\xi_1\times \xi_2 = q_1p_2 - q_2p_1\), then \[ [J_{\xi_1}, J_{\xi_2}] = 2i \sin \left( \dfrac\pi p \xi_1\times \xi_2 \right) J_{\xi_1+\xi_2} \]

Proceeding to compute the commutator, note that \(UV = \bar \omega VU\), then \[\begin{align} [J_{\xi_1}, J_{\xi_2}] &= [\bar \omega^{q_1p_1/2} V^{q_1} U^{p_1}, \bar \omega^{q_2 p_2/2} V^{q_2} U^{p_2}] \\ &= \omega^{-\frac 1 2 (q_1p_1 + q_2 p_2)} (V^{q_1} U^{p_1} V^{q_2} U^{p_2} - V^{q_2} U^{p_2}V^{q_1} U^{p_1}) \\ &= \omega^{-\frac 1 2 (q_1p_1 + q_2 p_2)} V^{q_1 + q_2} U^{p_1 + p_2} (\omega^{-p_1 q_2} - \omega^{-q_1p_2}) \tag{2.1} \end{align}\] On the other hand, reminding that \(\xi_1\times \xi_2 = q_1p_2 - q_2p_1\), we obtain \[\begin{align} 2i \sin \left(\dfrac\pi p \xi_1\times \xi_2 \right) J_{\xi_1+\xi_2} &= \left[\exp\left(\dfrac{i\pi}{p} \xi_1\times \xi_2\right) - \exp\left(-\dfrac{i\pi}{p} \xi_1\times \xi_2\right)\right] \omega^{-\frac 1 2 (q_1+q_2)(p_1+p_2)} V^{q_1+q_2}U^{p_1+p_2} \\ &= \left(\omega^{\xi_1\times \xi_2/2} - \omega^{-\xi_1\times \xi_2/2}\right) \omega^{-\frac 1 2 (q_1+q_2)(p_1+p_2)}V^{q_1+q_2} U^{p_1+p_2} \end{align}\] In the first term \(\omega^{\xi_1\times \xi_2/2}\), the \(+q_1p_2\) cancels with that from \(\omega^{-\frac 1 2 (q_1+q_2)(p_1+p_2)}\), leaving \(\omega^{-\frac 1 2 (q_1p_q + q_2p_2) - q_2p_1}\), matching the first term in (2.1), as desired. The same goes for the second term.

Proposition 2.2 (canonical decomposition of Weyl algebras) Let \(p=p_1p_2\) relative primes, then \[ W_{p_1}\otimes W_{p_2} \cong W_p \] under the mapping extended multiplicatively from the generators \[ \varphi\, U_{(p_1)} \otimes U_{(p_2)} = U_{(p)}, \quad \varphi\, V_{(p_1)} \otimes V_{(p_2)} = V_{(p)}^{(p_1+p_2)} \] Note that since \(p_1, p_2\) are relative primes, \(U\otimes U\) and \(V\otimes V\) does generate \(W_{p_1}\otimes W_{p_2}\). Explicitly, we obtain the embedding isomorphisms \[\begin{align} \varphi \, U^{p_2}_{(p_1)} \otimes U^{p_2}_{(p_2)} &= \varphi \, U^{p_2}_{(p_1)} \otimes 1 = U_{(p)}^{p_2} \\ \varphi \, V^{p_2}_{(p_1)} \otimes V^{p_2}_{(p_2)} &= \varphi \, V^{p_2}_{(p_1)} \otimes 1 = V_{(p)}^{(p_1+p_2)p_2} = V_{(p)}^{p_2^2} \end{align}\]

Weyl parafermions

Definition 2.2 (Weyl parafermion generators) Fixing \(p\) and \(N\), we can define an alternate set of generators of \(W_p(N)\) by \[ \Gamma_j = U_1\dots U_{j-1}V_j, \quad \Delta_j = U_1\dots U_{j-1} (U_j V_j) = \Gamma_j U_j \] Set \(\gamma_1, \dots \gamma_{2n} = \Gamma_1, \Delta_1, \Gamma_2, \Delta_2, \dots\), then we obtain \[ \gamma_j \gamma_k = [\delta_{jk} + (1-\delta_{jk}) \omega] \gamma_k \gamma_j, \quad \Gamma_j^p = \Delta_j^p = 1, \quad \gamma_j^\dagger= \gamma_j^{p-1} \] One can recover the Weyl generators by \[ U_j = \Gamma_j^\dagger\Delta_j, \quad V_j = \Gamma_j \prod_{k=1}^{j-1} U_k^\dagger = \Gamma_j \prod_{k=1}^{j-1} \Delta_k^\dagger\Gamma_k \]

For the case of \(p=2\), we obtain \(\Gamma_j = Z_1\dots Z_{j-1}X_j\) and \(\Delta_j = -iZ_1 \dots Z_{j-1}Y_j\).

For a more comprehensive treatment of duality, see (Cobanera, Ortiz, and Nussinov 2011).

Definition 2.3 (parafermion self-duality) We are interested in the following duality for the \(p\)-clock model (which has a transparant parafermion interpretation). Fixing \(N\) sites, consider the von-Neumann algebra \(\mathcal A\) generated by \(\{U_1, \dots, U_n, V_1V_2^\dagger, V_2V_3^\dagger, \dots, V_{N-1}V_N^\dagger, V_N\}\). Let \(r(j_{1\leq j\leq N}) = N+1-j\) (denoting the reverse order) and define \[\begin{align} \Phi_d(U_1) &= V^\dagger_N, \quad \Phi_d(V_N) = U_1 \\ \Phi_d(U_j) &= V^\dagger_{r(j)} V_{r(j)+1}, \quad \Phi_d(V_j V_{j+1}^\dagger) = U_{r(j)} \end{align}\]

Definition 2.4 (Artin braid group) The braid group \(B_L\) on \(L\) modes is the free group in generators \(\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_{L-1}\) subject to \(\sigma_k \sigma_{k+1}\sigma_k = \sigma_{k+1}\sigma_k \sigma_{k+1}\) and \(\sigma_k\sigma_l = \sigma_l\sigma_k\) if \(|k-l|\geq 2\).

Fock parafermions

Here, we construct the Fock space of indistinguishable particles upon specifying:

  1. The state space \(\mathcal H\) of a single particle (orbitals it can occupy).
  2. A rule to multiply \(N\) single-particle states to generate a \(N\)-body state with the correct exchange and exclusion statistics.

Let \(n_E\geq 2\) denote the exclusion parameter, the Fock space \(\mathcal F\) on \(M\) sites is \[ \mathcal F = \bigoplus_{N=0} \text{span of } |n_1, \dots, n_M\rangle\text{ subject to } \sum_{r=1}^M n_r = N \] We abbreviate the notation \(|n_j\rangle\) denotes \(n_j\) particles in the \(j\)-th entry, and \(|n_j, n_k\rangle\) with \((n_j, n_k)\) particles in the \((j, k)\)-th sites, and so on. We next proceed to make \(\mathcal F\) an algebra by specifying multiplication:

Proposition 2.3 (multiplication on Fock space) Define \(|1_j\rangle=|n_j=1\rangle\) and the following rules for multiplication:

  1. \(|1_{\forall j}\rangle^{n_E} = 0\), where \(n_E\) is the exclusion parameter.
  2. \(|n_1, \dots n_M\rangle= |1_1\rangle^{n_1} |1_2\rangle^{n_2} \dots |1_M\rangle^{n_M}\).
  3. \(|1_j\rangle|1_{k\neq j}\rangle= \omega |1_k\rangle|1_j\rangle\implies |n_j\rangle|n_k\rangle= \omega^{n_kn_j} |n_k\rangle|n_j\rangle= |n_j, n_k\rangle\) for \(j\leq k\).
  4. Every operator commuting with every other operator in \(\mathcal F\) is a scalar multiple of the Fock vacuum.

Definition 2.5 (creation and annihilation operators) Define the creation operator \(C_j^\dagger: \mathcal F\to \mathcal F\) by extending \[ C_j^\dagger|n_1, \dots, n_M\rangle= |1_j\rangle|n_1, \dots, n_M\rangle = \omega^{\sum_{k=1}^{j-1} n_k} |n_1, \dots, n_j+1, \dots, n_M\rangle \] In lieu of the given inner product, its adjoint annihilation operator \(C_j\) is \[\begin{align} \langle m_1, \dots, m_M| (C_j |n_1, \dots, n_M\rangle) &= (C_j^\dagger|m_1, \dots m_M\rangle)^\dagger|n_1, \dots, n_M\rangle\\ &= \omega^{\sum_{k=1}^{j-1} m_k} \langle m_1, \dots, m_j+1, m_M| n_1, \dots, n_M\rangle\\ &= \omega^{\sum_{k=1}^{j-1} n_k} \delta_{m_1n_1}\dots \delta_{(m_j+1)n_j} \dots \delta_{m_M n_M} \\ &= \omega^{\sum_{k=1}^{j-1} n_k}\delta_{m_1n_1}\dots \delta_{m_j(n_j-1)} \dots \delta_{m_M n_M} \\ C_j |n_1, \dots, n_M\rangle &= \omega^{\sum_{k=1}^{j-1} n_k}|n_1, \dots, n_j-1, n_M\rangle \end{align}\] The algebra of the creation and annihilation operators thus are \[\begin{align} C_j^{\dagger p} &= C_j^p = 0, \quad C_j^\dagger C_{k>j}^\dagger= \omega C_k^\dagger C_j^\dagger, \quad C_j C_k = \omega C_k C_j \end{align}\]

Low-dimensional Lie algebras

Focusing on the \(p=3\) case, let \(\omega = \exp \left(2\pi i/3\right)\) and label the majorana operators \(\gamma_1, \dots, \gamma_{2n}\) as usual, subject to the algebraic relations \[ \gamma_j^3 = 1, \quad \gamma_j^\dagger= \gamma_j^2, \quad \gamma_j \gamma_k = \omega^{s_{jk}} \gamma_k \gamma_j, \quad s_{jk} = \begin{cases} 1 & j < k \\ -1 & j>k \\ 0 & j = k \end{cases} \] Consider operators \(\Gamma_{jk} = \gamma_j \gamma_k^2\). First note that operators of the form \(\Gamma_{jk} + \Gamma_{kj}\) is Hermitian: \[ \Gamma_{jk}^\dagger = \gamma_k^{2\dagger}\gamma_j^\dagger = \gamma_k \gamma_j^2 = \Gamma_{kj} \] Next compute the commutator brackets: for \(j, k, l\) distinct, we obtain \[\begin{align} [\Gamma_{jk}, \Gamma_{kl}] &= \gamma_j \gamma_k^2 \gamma_k \gamma_l^2 - \gamma_k \gamma_l^2 \gamma_j \gamma_k^2 = (1 - \omega^{2s_{kl} + s_{kj} + 2s_{lj}}) \Gamma_{jl} \end{align}\]
