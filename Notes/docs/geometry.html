1 Geometry

Riemannian Space

Definition 1.1 (Poincare half-plane model) The \(n\)-dimensional hyperbolic space is defined as \[ \mathbb H^n = \{(x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, y) | y\geq 0\} \] Equipped with the Riemannian metric \[ ds^2 = \dfrac 1 {y^2} \left(dy^2 + \sum_j dx_j^2\right) \]

Definition 1.2 (Poincare ball model) \(\mathbb H^n = B_1(0)\subset \mathbb R^n\) with metric \[ ds^2 = 4\dfrac{\sum dx_j^2}{(1-|x|^2)^2} \]

Definition 1.3 (hyperboloid model) \(\mathbb H^n = \{(x_1, \dots, x_n, x_{n+1})| \langle x, x\rangle_M = 1\}\), where \(\langle\cdot, \cdot\rangle_M\) is the Minkowski metric, with metric given by the pullback metric.

Proposition 1.1 (geodesics in half-plane model) Consider \(\mathbb H^2\) in the ball model, the geodesic equations are derived by extremizing the length functional with Lagrangian given by the metric. Omitting the factor of \(4\): \[ \mathcal L = \dfrac{\dot x_1^2 + \dot x_2^2}{(1-x_1^2-x_2^2)^2} \]

Hyperbolic Spaces

Basic definitions

Definition 1.4 (geodesic metric space) A metric space \((X, d)\) is geodesic if every two points \(x, y\) is joined by a path \(\overline{xy}\) with length equal to \(d(x, y)\).

Definition 1.5 (hyperbolic space) A geodesic metric space \((X, d)\) is \(\delta\)-hyperbolic, for \(\delta \geq 0\), if for every \(x, y, z\in X\), we have \[ \overline{xy} \subset B_\delta(\overline{zx})\cup B_\delta(\overline{zy}) \] The space is hyperbolic if \(\delta\)-hyperbolic for some \(\delta>0\).

  • Any tree with length distance is \(0\)-hyperbolic. In such ideal hyperbolic spaces, geodesic triangles are tripods.
  • Standard hyperbolic plane \(\mathbb H^2\) is \(\log(\sqrt 2+1)\)-hyperbolic .

Definition 1.6 (hyperbolic group) A finitely generated group \(G\) is word-hyperbolic if there is a finite generating set \(S\subset G\) such that the Cayley graph \(\Gamma(G, S)\) is hyperbolic w.r.t. the word metric \(d_S\).

Definition 1.7 (Gromov product) Given a metric space \((X, d)\) and \(x, y, z\in X\), the Gromov product is defined as \[ (x, y)_z = \dfrac{ d(x, z) + d(y, z) - d(x, y) } 2 \]

  • In the tree-case, the Gromov product is always \(0\). The product measures the precision of the triangle inequality by \[ d(x, y) \leq d(x, z) + d(y, z) \iff 0 \leq 2 (x, y)_z \]
  • Again, in the tree-case, \((x, y)_z\) measures how long \(\overline{zx}, \overline{zy}\) travel before diverging.

Definition 1.8 (Hausdorff distance) Given \(A, B\subset X\), the Hausdorff distance is \[ d_H(A, B) = \inf\left\{ \epsilon | A\subset B_\epsilon(B), B\subset B_\epsilon(A) \right\} \] It measures the minimal amount we have to expand \(A, B\) by for them to contain each other.

Proposition 1.2 In a \(\delta\)-hyperbolic space \((X, d)\):

  1. The initial segments of length \((y, z)_x\) of \(\overline{xy}\) and \(\overline{xz}\) are \(2\delta\)-Hausdorff close.
  2. \((y, z)_x\) approximates within \(2\delta\) the distance from \(x\) to \(\overline{yz}\).

Definition 1.9 (equivalence of geodesic rays) Two geodesic rays \(\gamma_1, \gamma_2:[0, \infty)\to X\) are equivalent, written \(\gamma_1\sim \gamma_2\), if \(\exists K>0\) such that \[ d(\gamma_1(\forall t), \gamma_2(t)) \leq K \] Transitivity follows from the triangle inequality, so \(\sim\) is indeed an equivalence relation.

Proposition 1.3 (equivalent characterization of geodesic equivalence) \(\gamma_1\sim \gamma_2\) iff their images have finite Hausdorff distance.

Boundary

Definition 1.10 (geodesic boundary) Given a \(\delta\)-hyperbolic space and base-point \(x\in X\),

  • The relative geodesic boundary of \(X\) w.r.t. \(x\) is \[ \partial_{x}^g X = \{\text{geodesic rays with } \gamma(0)=x\} / \sim \]
  • The geodesic boundary of \(X\) is \(\partial^g X = \text{geodesic rays} / \sim\).

Definition 1.11 (convergence to infinity) Given a base-point \(x\in X\) in a hyperbolic metric space, a sequence \((x_n)\) of points converges to infinity if \[ \liminf_{i, j\to \infty} (x_i, x_j)_x = \infty \]
